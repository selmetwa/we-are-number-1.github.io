We're number 1!

Many of us in the United States grow up assuming that we live in the richest, most powerful, freest, and overall greatest country in the world.

I remember watching Obama’s 2013 inaugural address where he said “America is still number one”(The “still” of course hints at some anxiety that there may be some slippage).

If we’re number one, what exactly are we number one in? Intrigued by this question, I decided to create this project and allow users to compare countries and continents based on a whole host of different metrics.

Interested by these results, particularly the score regarding Wealth Inequality, I wanted to do a more in-depth comparison with other OCED countries on metrics that I found personally interesting.

I added 4 new metrics specific to OCED countries including, collective bargaining score, trade union participation, annual hours per worker, and public expenditure as a percent of GDP.

I then made a scatterplot to see if workers rights (collective bargaining, trade union participation) is a good indicator of wealth inequality, the minimum wage, and annual hours worked.

Interesting takeaways from this scatterplot:

Collective Bargaining vs Annual Hours

Based on this scatterplot there appears to be a negative correlation between the collective bargaining rights of workers compared to the amount of hours they work annually

This means the higher the bargaining rights of workers the less hours they work annually.

It is also interesting to note that the United States is more similar to Turkey, Poland, and Estonia, than Western/Northern Europe.

Trade Unions vs Lowest Hourly Wage

There is a slight positive correlation between trade union membership and the lowest hourly wage of a country.

The obvious outlier is Switzerland which has a relatively low number of workers in trade unions but has the highest minimum wage of any country.

Tax Rate vs Wealth Inequality

There appears to be a strong negative correlation between the amount of taxes a country pays compared with the amount of wealth inequality in that country. Higher taxes generally means less wealth inequality.

The United States again ranks much closer to Mexico, Turkey, and Chile than Western/Northern Europe.

Country Correlation Map

Select a country from the dropdown menu to see which other countries are closely related. The map will recolor itself to display the most similar countries and the least similar countries.

The correlation was done by creating an array filled with the percentile scores of each metric for every country, and then comparing every array to the target country using the Pearson Correlation Coefficient (PCC). The numbers you see when hovering over a country represents each countries score from the PCC.

Note: the metrics exclusive to OCED countries were excluded from these calculations, as the results would be skewed too much.

Key takeaways from this map:

Most countries are similar to nearby countries

A country like Egypt is closely correlated to most of Africa and the rest of the Arab world. The same trend applies to Western Europe, most of South America, and most of Asia.

Some countries are not similar to nearby countries

Countries like Japan and Korea and more related to Western Europe & North America than China, and Southeast Asia. Also Uruguay is unlike most of South America and again resembles Western Europe more than neighboring countries.

Some countries are oddly unique

A few countries, most notably, Tunisia and Mongolia, are not closely correlated to any other countries on the map.
